Sorting State AI Laws · Figure 1

Bucket-Count Summary

193 Enacted State AI Laws Sorted by Regulatory Modality

This figure presents the empirical distribution of 193 enacted state AI statutes across four regulatory-modality buckets. The taxonomy — Process, Output, Conduct, and Mixed — is the author’s original analytical contribution and is described in § III.C of the accompanying Comment.

Fig. 1 — 193 enacted state AI laws by regulatory-modality bucket

Roughly 31% of the dataset is in the bucket already losing in court under Kohls v. Bonta. Roughly 16% is the bucket the Comment defends. Roughly 43% is conduct that the Executive Order’s own § 8(b) carve-outs already preserve.

Conduct 84 statutes · 43.5%

Acts already unlawful regardless of AI involvement. The Executive Order's own § 8(b) carve-outs (child safety, AI computing infrastructure, state government procurement) overlap heavily with this bucket.

Output 60 statutes · 31.1%

Statutes that condition the lawfulness of AI communicative product on its form or content. Compelled disclaimers, watermarking mandates, and content-based prohibitions are typical. Carries live First Amendment risk under Kohls v. Bonta.

Process 31 statutes · 16.1%

Statutes governing how AI systems are developed, documented, and managed. Lead examples include TRAIGA, the Colorado AI Act, TFAIA, and the RAISE Act.

Mixed 18 statutes · 9.3%

Statutes that genuinely blend modalities and resist clean sorting. Healthcare AI is the dominant pattern; ten of the eighteen mixed statutes regulate healthcare AI.
